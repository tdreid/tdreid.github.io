Blockchain's Big Deal Made Simple

A few weeks ago a long time acquaintance and graphic designer, who has installed some of the coolest murals around in my hometown, posed a great question about blockchain.

I’d sum up the way Rory Allen put it this way. A generation ago we all got excited about the internet, e-commerce and the dot com trend. Not everybody totally understood what all the fuss was about, some were downright skeptical. But of course — here we are in 2020.

Today it feels like we’re probably in the same spot with blockchain as back then with the web. Once again, not everybody gets it. But it’s clear from the last go round that getting “it” — whatever “it” is at any given moment — is super important.

So, can anyone succinctly explain why blockchain is so revolutionary?

Wow. I’d been there in that era, and recalled the electricity, the thrill of knowing not just that life was changing but also why and how (kinda). Somehow, for reasons I can’t really take credit for I got “it”. As a result, “it” made me rich, then poor, then rich again — a couple times over, teaching valuable lessons all along the way.

The thing, though, is that after finding my comfort zone as a web developer I hadn’t really felt the same atmosphere of grit and excitement for while, a decade or more. That changed recently as I learned to build apps on blockchain.

For the first time since the advent of the dot com era I realized that life was changing again. And also why and how (kinda). Like the automobile and the web before it, blockchain is becoming key to the lives of people who don’t neccesarily know how it runs.

Coming across Rory’s question was very affirming of my nerdy interest in chains. A growing number of people share that perspective and curiosity. To honor that excitement, I wanted to take a shot at answering Rory’s question.

Blockchains help us solve a problem inherent in what makes networked computers useful in the first place: making perfect copies extremely fast.

Right now in fact you’re reading a perfect copy of something I typed on my device some time ago and probably far away from you.

While this is useful for some things it is very dangerous for high stakes systems that rely on trust (like currency, or elections).That’s because if such a system is compromised in a way that allows counterfeits the forged elements will appear “perfect”. With an ordinary digital record it’s impossible to be certain whether or not tampering has occurred.

Up until now, we’ve usually turned to some mutually trusted central authority such as a bank, escrow agent or government to oversee such digital transactions.

That’s where distributed blockchains are an alternative. Each block in a blockchain is cryptographically linked to its predecessor. If changes are made after the fact the math underlying the encryption fails and forgeries/tampering can be detected.

The validity of an open blockchain ledger like Bitcoin can be checked by any participant at any time. The huge number of anonymous strangers that would have to cooperate in a forgery is impractical, making a blockchain tamper resistant.

This simple breakthrough in turn has huge implications for all sytems that have relied on centralized trust: currency, contracts, banking, games, securities, and supply chains to name a few.

The vast rewards, as always, come along with new challenges and risks.
